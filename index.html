Abstract

Formula One is a sport know as the pinnacle of motorsport racing, however most peaople dont understand the significance of the egineering behind it.
This paper will explore the physics behind aerodynamic designs in modern F1 racing cars and how airflow is utilised to make the car as 
optimal as possible when taking sharp corners at dangerousily high speeds.

Introduction

  
Formula One is an extremely competitive sport in which even the smallest design changes to a car can have a significant impact 
on its performance and known as the epitome of racing in modern day motorsports. It consists of highspeed sharp turns that 
require the cars to be heavily optimized aerodynamicaly, to generate as much downforce as possible without sacrificing speed. 
Every second counts and so the manipulation of airflow around the vehicles heavily affect the speed and stability of the car 
which can be the difference between winning or losing. As a result of this teams invest millions to innovate new technology,
to push the limits and reach record breaking speeds.

Analysis and discussion

Front Wing
The front wing manipulates airflow and creates downforce, creating better stability and grip for the car. The front wing is an inverted airfoil which forces air flowing over the surface on top to be slowed. This creates high pressure on top whilst on the bottom the faster moving air creates low pressure, this difference in pressure creates a net downforce. Modern designs also use the wing to direct air from the front wheels, to seal the underbody and protect the car behind.
"As shown in the sketch in Fig. 1, the geometry consists of a multi-element wing in ground effect, leading to the generation of a series of interacting vortical structures, thereby creating a highly complex flow environment downstream of the wing. Accurately modelling these vortices and their interactions, by capturing their relative position and strength, is of utmost importance for a successful front wing design."

Rear Wing
The back wing is very similar to the front wing, however is shaped like an inverted airplane wing and has a special feature called DRS(drag reduction system). The DRS is a moveable flap that the drivers can open to reduce drag by letting airflow through the rear wing. What it does is reduce the area the air has to pass over the car, reducing the drag and the downforce. This allows the drivers to reach incredible speeds on the straights.
Chassis stiffness
A stiff chassis provides a solid foundation for the suspension to work from. It ensures that when the suspension moves, the intended forces are applied to the tires, and the chassis doesn't twist and absorb those forces instead. This improves the handling of the vehicle by making the suspension more effective, which in turn creates more traction leading to sharper steering, Furthermore when a driver turns the wheel, a stiffer chassis inputs into the suspension and tires more quickly and accurately. This results in a more direct and responsive feel. It is usually found that a chassis that has enough torsional stiffness usually has "ample rigidity in bending; as a consequence torsional rigidity represents the main criterion of chassis design, and the primary function of a high-performance chassis" Sampo, E. (2013). Modelling chassis flexibility in vehicle dynamics simulation.
